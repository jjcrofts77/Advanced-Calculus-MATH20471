6.3 Evaluation of real integrals¶

It is quite surprising that certain classes of complicated real integrals can be integrated by the residue theorem, as we shall see.

In this course we shall consider real integrals of the type

\[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\mathrm{d}x. \]

Such an integral is called an improper integral, and it has the meaning

(56)¶\[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\mathrm{d}x = \lim_{a\rightarrow\infty}\int_a^0 f(x)\mathrm{d}x + \lim_{b\rightarrow\infty}\int_0^b f(x)\mathrm{d}x. \]

If both limits exist then we write

(57)¶\[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\mathrm{d}x = \lim_{R\rightarrow\infty}\int_{-R}^R f(x)\mathrm{d}x. \]

The limit in eqreflabel2) is called the Cauchy principal value of the integral. It is written

\[ \mathrm{P} \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\mathrm{d}x. \]

It may exist even if the limits in eqreflabel1 do not. For example,

\[ \lim_{R\rightarrow\infty}\int_{-R}^R x\mathrm{d}x = \lim_{R\rightarrow\infty}\left(\frac{R^2}{2}-\frac{R^2}{2}\right) = 0, \quad\text{but}\quad \lim_{b\rightarrow\infty}\int_0^b x\mathrm{d}x = \infty. \]

Now, we assume that the function \(f(x)\) is a real rational function, i.e.

\[ f(x) =\frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}, \]

whose denominator is non-zero for all real \(x\) and is of degree at least 2 units higher than the degree of the numerator. Note that \(P\) and \(Q\) are polynomials in \(x\). Examples of such functions include

\[ \frac{1}{x^2}, ~ \frac{x^2+1}{x^4+1} ~\text{ and }~ \frac{1}{(x^2-2x+5)^2}. \]

In particular, for such functions we have that the limits in eqreflabel1 and eqreflabel2 exist.

Fig. 34 Path \(\mathcal{C}\) of the contour integral in the example below¶

Consider the corresponding contour integral (\(f\) the same as above)

\[ \oint_\mathcal{C}f(z)\mathrm{d}z \]

around the path \(\mathcal{C}\) in Figure 8. Since \(f(x)\) is rational, \(f(z)\) has finitely many poles in the upper half-plane, and if we choose \(R\) large enough, then \(\mathcal{C}\) encloses all these poles. By the residue theorem we then obtain

(58)¶\[ \oint_\mathcal{C}f(z)\mathrm{d}z = \int_Sf(z)\mathrm{d}z + \int_{-R}^Rf(x)\mathrm{d}x = 2\pi i\sum_j \mathrm{Res}(f; z_j), \]

where the sum consists of all residues of \(f(z)\) at the points in the upper half-plane at which \(f(z)\) has a pole.

From eqreflabel3 we have

(59)¶\[ \int_{-R}^Rf(x)\mathrm{d}x = 2\pi i\sum_j \mathrm{Res}(f;z_j) - \int_Sf(z)\mathrm{d}z. \]

Importantly, it can be shown that (using the ML-inequality, which is outside the scope of the current course) in the \(R\rightarrow\infty\) limit the integral over the semicircle \(S\) approaches zero, i.e.

\[ \int_Sf(z)\mathrm{d}z\rightarrow 0 \text{ as } R\rightarrow\infty, \]

and so in the \(R\rightarrow\infty\) limit eqreflabel4 yields the result

(60)¶\[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\mathrm{d}x = 2\pi i\sum_j \mathrm{Res}(f;z_j).\]

Here the sum consists of all residues of \(f(z)\) at the points in the upper half-plane at which \(f(z)\) has a pole.


Example 6.3.1 Show that

\[ \int_0^\infty \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{1+x^4} = \frac{\pi}{2\sqrt{2}}. \]

Solution

The function \(\displaystyle f(x)=1/(1+x^4)\) is a rational function and has a denominator 4 units higher than its numerator and so we can apply the above techniques to solve the integral.

Fig. 35 Singularities of \(\displaystyle f(z) = 1/(1+z^4)\)¶

Indeed, \(\displaystyle f(z)=1/(1+z^4)\) has 4 order 1 poles at the points (see the sketch)

\[ z_1 = e^{\pi i/4},\quad z_2 = e^{3\pi i/4},\quad z_3 = e^{-3\pi i/4}, \quad z_4 = e^{-\pi i/4}. \]

The first two poles lie in the upper half-plane. From earlier we have that

\[\begin{split} \mathrm{Res}(f; z_1) &=\left[\frac{1}{\left(1+z^4\right)'}\right]_{z=z_1} = \left.\frac{1}{4z^3}\right|_{z=z_1} = \frac{1}{4}e^{-3\pi i/4} = -\frac{1}{4}e^{\pi i/4}\\ \mathrm{Res}(f; z_2) &=\left[\frac{1}{\left(1+z^4\right)'}\right]_{z=z_2} = \left.\frac{1}{4z^3}\right|_{z=z_2} = \frac{1}{4}e^{-9\pi i/4} = \frac{1}{4}e^{-\pi i/4}. \end{split}\]

Here we have used the fact that \(\displaystyle e^{\pi i} = -1\) and \(\displaystyle e^{-2\pi i}=1\).

From eqreflabel5 we have

\[ \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{\mathrm{d}x}{1+x^4} = -\frac{2\pi i}{4}\left(e^{\pi i/4}-e^{-\pi i/4}\right) = -\frac{2\pi i}{4}\cdot 2i\cdot\sin\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right) = \frac{\pi}{\sqrt{2}}. \]

Since \(\displaystyle 1/(1+x^4)\) is an even function, we thus obtain, as asserted,

\[ \int_0^\infty\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{1+x^4} = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-\infty}^\infty\frac{\mathrm{d}x}{1+x^4} = \frac{\pi}{2\sqrt{2}}. \]
