4.5 The annulus theorem¶

The following is a useful result which can simplify integrating around a singularity.

If \(f(z)\) is analytic on the closed annulus determined by the two simple closed paths \(\mathscr{C}_1\) and \(\mathscr{C}_2\) then

\[ \oint_{\mathscr{C}_1} f(z)\mathrm{d} z = \oint_{\mathscr{C}_2} f(z)\mathrm{d} z, \]

provided that the two paths are traversed in the same direction.

Fig. 31 An annulus formed from two circular paths \(\mathscr{C}_1\) and \(\mathscr{C}_2\) cut via additional paths \(\gamma_1\) and \(\gamma_2\) separated by a small distance \(\epsilon\to 0\) to leave new paths \(\mathscr{C}^*_1\) and \(\mathscr{C}^*_2\).¶

This can be shown by splitting the annulus via two additional paths \(\gamma_1\) and \(\gamma_2\) as shown in the figure. Note, the paths in this figure do not have to be circular. The paths \(\mathscr{C}_1\) and \(\mathscr{C}_2\) are therefore split to new paths denoted \(\mathscr{C}^*_1\) and \(\mathscr{C}^*_2\).

Now, considering the integral around the entire shape as indicated by the arrows:

\[\begin{split} I & = & \int_{\mathscr{C}^*_1}f(z)\mathrm{d} z + \int_{\gamma_1} f(z)\mathrm{d} z - \int_{\mathscr{C}^*_2}f(z)\mathrm{d} z + \int_{\gamma_2}f(z)\mathrm{d} z\\ &=& 0\ \mbox{due to Cauchy's theorem} \end{split}\]

Note that the minus sign in the integral around \(\mathscr{C}^*_2\) is because the integral is taken in the opposite direction to \(\mathscr{C}^*_2\) indicated in the figure.

In the limit \(\epsilon\to 0\), \(\gamma_2\) is the opposite path to \(\gamma_1\) (so that \(\gamma_1\to -\gamma_2\)):

\[ \int_{\gamma_1} f(z)\mathrm{d} z=-\int_{\gamma_2} f(z)\mathrm{d} z\ \]

so that those two integrals cancel in \(I\). Also, in this limit, the \(\mathscr{C}^*_1\to \mathscr{C}_1\) and \(\mathscr{C}^*_2\to \mathscr{C}_2\) and

\[ \int_{\mathscr{C}^*_1}f(z)\mathrm{d} z = \oint_{\mathscr{C}_1}f(z)\mathrm{d} z,\ \int_{\mathscr{C}^*_2}f(z)\mathrm{d} z = \oint_{\mathscr{C}_2}f(z)\mathrm{d} z,\ \]

then

\[ I = \oint_{\mathscr{C}_1}f(z)\mathrm{d} z-\oint_{\mathscr{C}_2}f(z)\mathrm{d} z=0 \]

which gives the required result for any simple closed paths \(\mathscr{C}_1\) and \(\mathscr{C}_2\).

Note

This theorem implies that, instead of integrating a function with a single singularity around a complicated closed path (avoiding the singularity), one can integrate the function around a circle with radius chosen such that the circle lies wholly within that path. This can greatly simplify the calculation.

Example 4.4.1 Evaluate \(\displaystyle I=\oint_\mathscr{C} \frac{1}{z-i} \mathrm{d} z\) where the path \(\mathscr{C}\) is a square centred on \(z=0\) with side 4.

The given function \(f(z)=\frac{1}{z-i}\) has a pole at \(z=i\) which lies within \(\mathscr{C}\). The integration can be simplified by replacing \(\mathscr{C}\) with a circle centred at \(z=i\) of radius \(r<1\); i.e.:

\[ \mathscr{C}^*: |z-i| = r. \]

Then, using the annulus theorem

\[ I=\oint_\mathscr{C} \frac{1}{z-i} \mathrm{d} z=\oint_{\mathscr{C}^*} \frac{1}{z-i} \mathrm{d} z=\oint_{|z-i|=r} \frac{1}{z-i} \mathrm{d} z \]

Now, in a previous example in these lectures we showed that integrating \(z^{-n}\) around the unit circle gives:

\[\begin{split} \oint z^{-n} \mathrm{d} z = \begin{cases} & \int_0^{2\pi}e^{-in\theta}\ ie^{i\theta}\mathrm{d}\theta = \int_0^{2\pi} i \mathrm{d} \theta = 2i\pi \ \mbox{if}\ n=1\\ & \int_0^{2\pi} e^{-in\theta}\ ie^{i\theta}\mathrm{d}\theta = i \int_0^{2\pi} e^{i(1-n)\theta} \mathrm{d} \theta = \frac{1}{(1-n)}\left( e^{2i\pi(1-n)}-1\right)=0\ \mbox{if}\ n\neq 1, \end{cases} \end{split}\]

and the same is true for the more general case \(f(z)=\frac{1}{z-a}\). (prove this to yourself.)

So, we can read off the answer:

\[ I=\oint_\mathscr{C} \frac{1}{z-i} \mathrm{d} z=\oint_{|z-i|=r} \frac{1}{z-i} \mathrm{d} z=2i\pi. \]

The multiple annulus theorem¶

By an analogous argument, it is simple to show that the annulus theorem can be extended to a multiple annulus.

If \(\mathscr{C}_1,\ \mathscr{C}_2,\ \mathscr{C}_3,\ldots,\mathscr{C}_n,\ldots \mathscr{C}\) are \(n+1\) simple closed paths such that:

  1. \(\mathscr{C}_i\), \(i=1,\ldots,n\) lie within \(\mathscr{C}\);

  2. Each \(\mathscr{C}_i\) lie in the exterior of \(\mathscr{C}_j\) for \(i\neq j\); that is, the interior simple closed paths \(\mathscr{C}_i\) do not intersect.

The group of paths \(\mathscr{C}_i, \mathscr{C}\) (\(i=1,\ldots,n\)) form a closed multiple annulus.

Fig. 32 A multiple annulus enclosing singularities at \(z=-1\) and \(z=2i\).¶

Then, if \(f(z)\) is analytic on a closed multiple annulus determined by the closed paths \(\mathscr{C}_1,\ \mathscr{C}_2,\ \mathscr{C}_3,\ldots,\mathscr{C}_n,\ \mathscr{C}\), then

\[ \oint_{\mathscr{C}}f(z)\mathrm{d} z = \oint_{\mathscr{C}_1}f(z)\mathrm{d} z + \oint_{\mathscr{C}_2}f(z)\mathrm{d} z + \ldots + \oint_{\mathscr{C}_n}f(z)\mathrm{d} z, \]

provided that the \(n\) paths are traversed in the same direction. This theorem is useful in the same way as above; i.e. if \(f\) has multiple singularities within \(\mathscr{C}\).

Example 4.4.2 Consider the integral:

\[ \oint_\mathscr{C}\frac{3}{z+1}+\frac{4}{z-2i} \mathrm{d} z \]

where the path \(\mathscr{C}\) is the circle \(|z|=4\) traversed in an anticlockwise direction.

Clearly the integrand fails to be analytic at \(z=-1\) and \(z=2i\). Each singularity may be isolated by choosing two interior paths \(\mathscr{C}_1\), \(\mathscr{C}_2\) such that:

\[ \mathscr{C}_1:\ |z+1|=1\ \mbox{and}\ \mathscr{C}_2:\ |z-2i|=1\, \]

as illustrated in the above Figure. Then, on the multiple closed annulus, the integrand is analytic since the singularities have been removed and the theorem gives:

\[\begin{split} \oint_{\mathscr{C}} \frac{3}{z+1}+\frac{4}{z-2i} \mathrm{d} z&=& \oint_{\mathscr{C}_1}\frac{3}{z+1}+\frac{4}{z-2i} \mathrm{d} z + \oint_{\mathscr{C}_2}\frac{3}{z+1}+\frac{4}{z-2i} \mathrm{d} z \\ &=& \oint_{\mathscr{C}_1}\frac{3}{z+1} \mathrm{d} z + \oint_{\mathscr{C}_2}\frac{3}{z+1} \mathrm{d} +\oint_{\mathscr{C}_1}\frac{4}{z+2i} \mathrm{d} z + \oint_{\mathscr{C}_2}\frac{4}{z-2i} \mathrm{d} z. \end{split}\]

Now, \(\frac{1}{z-2i}\) is analytic within and on \(\mathscr{C}_1\) and \(\frac{3}{z+1}\) is analytic within and on \(\mathscr{C}_2\) so by Cauchy’s theorem, the second and third integrals are zero. The integral reduces to:

\[ \oint_{\mathscr{C}} \frac{3}{z+1}+\frac{4}{z-2i} \mathrm{d} z = \oint_{\mathscr{C}_1}\frac{3}{z+1} \mathrm{d} z + \oint_{\mathscr{C}_2}\frac{4}{z-2i} \mathrm{d} z. \]

Employing the results we derived earlier:

\[\begin{split} \oint_\mathscr{C} (z-a)^{-n} \mathrm{d} z = \begin{cases} & 2i\pi \ \mbox{if}\ n=1\\ & 0\ \mbox{if}\ n\neq 1, \end{cases} \end{split}\]

when integrated around a unit circle centred on \(a\), we can evaluate the above integral as

\[ \oint_{\mathscr{C}_1}\frac{3}{z+1} \mathrm{d} z + \oint_{\mathscr{C}_2}\frac{4}{z-2i} \mathrm{d} z=3(2i\pi )+4(2i\pi )=14i\pi. \]
