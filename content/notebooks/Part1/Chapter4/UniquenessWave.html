4.4 Uniqueness of an IBVP for a Finite String (optional)¶

We consider the wave equation

(33)¶\[ \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = c^2\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}, \text{ for } 0<x<L, t>0, \]

and prove a uniqueness theorem based on energy considerations. The kinetic energy of the string is

\[ \frac{1}{2}\int_0^L\rho\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\right)^2\mathrm{d}x. \]

The stress energy is the product of the tension and the extension, where the extension is

\[\begin{split} \int_0^L\sqrt{1+\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)^2}\mathrm{d}x-L&=\int_0^L\left[1+\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)^2+\cdots\right]\mathrm{d}x-L,\\ &\approx \frac{1}{2}\int_0^L\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)^2\mathrm{d}x. \end{split}\]

Thus

(34)¶\[ E(t):=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^L\left[\rho\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\right)^2+T\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)^2\right]\mathrm{d}x, \]

is the energy of the string. The energy appears to depend upon time but in important cases it is in fact constant.


Lemma 4.4.1 If \(y(x,t)\) is a solution of the wave equation (33) and satisfies the boundary conditions

(35)¶\[ y(0,t)=0 \text{ and } y(L,t)=0 \text{ for } t>0, \]

then \(E(t)\) is constant for \(t\geq 0\).

Proof

Leibniz’s rule applied to (34) gives

\[ E'(t) = \int_0^L\left[\rho\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2}+T\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x\partial t}\right]\mathrm{d}x, \]

and, on substituting for \(\rho\partial^2y/\partial t^2\) from the wave equation (33), we find that

\[\begin{split} E'(t)&=T\int_0^L\left[\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}+\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x\partial t}\right]\mathrm{d}x\\ &=T\int_0^L\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\right)\mathrm{d}x\\ &=T\left[\frac{\partial y}{\partial x}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}\right]_0^L, \end{split}\]

hence \(E'(t)=0\) since the boundary conditions (35) tell us that \(\partial y/\partial t(0,t)=0\) and \(\partial y/\partial t(L,t)=0\) for \(t\geq 0\). Thus \(E(t)\) is constant. \(\quad\blacksquare\)


Theorem 4.4.2 (Uniqueness) For each pair of functions \(f\) and \(g\), the IBVP

\[ \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = c^2\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial x^2}, \text{ for } 0<x<L, t>0, \]
\[ y(x,0)=f(x) \text{ and } \frac{\partial y}{\partial t}(x,0) = g(x) \text{ for } 0\leq x\leq L, \]
\[ y(0,t)=0 \text{ and } y(L,t)=0 \text{ for } t>0, \]

has at most one solution. (That a solution exists we know since we have constructed a solution with the aid of separation of variables and Fourier series.)


Proof

Let \(y(x,t)\) and \(u(x,t)\) both be solutions of the IBVP. Consider the difference \(w:=y-u\) and the associated energy

\[ E(t):=\frac{1}{2}\int_0^L\left[\rho\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial t}\right)^2 + T\left(\frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\right)^2\right]\mathrm{d}x. \]

Note that \(E(t)\geq 0\). Now \(w(x,t)\) is a solution of the IBVP

\[ \frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial t^2} = c^2\frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial x^2}, \text{ for } 0<x<L, t>0, \]
(36)¶\[ w(x,0)=0 \text{ and } \frac{\partial w}{\partial t}(x,0) = 0 \text{ for } 0\leq x\leq L, \]
(37)¶\[ w(0,t)=0 \text{ and } w(L,t)=0 \text{ for } t>0. \]

By Lemma 4.4.1 and the boundary conditions (37), \(E(t)\equiv\text{ constant}\), and, in view of the initial conditions (36), \(E(0)=0\). Hence \(E(t)=0\) for every \(t>0\). Thus \(\partial w/\partial x=0\), \(\partial w/\partial t=0\) and \(w(x,t)\) is independent of both \(x\) and \(t\). Using (37) again tells us that \(w(x,t)\equiv 0\). Thus \(y=u\) and the solution is unique. \(\quad \blacksquare\)
